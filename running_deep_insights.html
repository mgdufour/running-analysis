But PRIME readiness doesn't mean unlimited — it means you were capable of a maximal output, which is exactly what happened. The crash that followed was proportional to the peak.

AGGRAVATING 5 — ACWR missed it. HRV caught it.

The 4-week vs 8-week load ratio stayed in the safe 0.9–1.2 range throughout — it never triggered a warning. ACWR measures volume, not density, so the clustering of back-to-back race-pace efforts within each week was invisible to it.

Garmin's biological sensors told a different story. HRV declined steadily from ~59ms (Army Run day) to 48ms (Oct 28) — a 19% drop over 37 days while training continued. In October alone: 11 of 28 days were rated LOW or POOR readiness (Oct 2, 3, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12, 13, 14, 27, 28). The body sent the signal repeatedly. It wasn't acted on.

This is why the 2026 plan uses both ACWR and HRV as injury guards — neither alone is sufficient. ACWR catches load spikes; HRV catches cumulative fatigue that volume metrics miss.
